Subject: The protein number is not the whole protein story

Preheader: DIAAS, amino acid quality, and why a 25g protein claim can hide a lot of label detail.

# Protein grams are not the same as protein quality

The scoop looks precise: one serving, 25g protein, a confident number on the front. The back of the pack is quieter. Two tubs can make the same claim and tell a very different label story. That gap is the point. It may tell you the protein source. It usually does not show how much of the key amino acid mix is easy to digest. DIAAS is the missing score.

**CleanLabel verdict:** A protein label is more trustworthy when the gram claim is backed by the protein source, a full amino acid profile, and clear third-party testing. The front-of-pack number is only the start of the story.

## The Science

The older protein score is PDCAAS, short for Protein Digestibility-Corrected Amino Acid Score. It adjusts raw protein for amino acid mix and digestion, but it uses whole-gut digestion and caps scores at 1.0. In 2013, an FAO expert group recommended DIAAS, the Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid Score. DIAAS looks at each essential amino acid at the ileum, the end of the small intestine.

The difference is not cosmetic, but the evidence needs the right label. A 2017 controlled study in the *British Journal of Nutrition* used growing pigs, not humans, to compare dairy and plant proteins. Mathai, Liu, and Stein reported that DIAAS split protein sources more clearly than PDCAAS-like scores. PDCAAS can rate some plant proteins too high and hide the top end of high-scoring dairy proteins.

The score map below uses published DIAAS examples, not a universal rank for every product.

| Protein source | Example DIAAS signal | Reading note |
|---|---:|---|
| Whey protein isolate | 109 | High digestibility and amino acid density in the cited pig-model data. |
| Soy protein isolate | 90 | One of the stronger plant isolate examples in the published score set. |
| Pea protein concentrate | 82 | Useful, especially in blends; often limited by sulfur amino acids. |
| Red lentils | 54 | A 2018 *Food Chemistry* lentil study reported 0.54 after cooking and processing. |
| Rice protein concentrate | <75 | Often lysine-limited; the amino acid profile matters more than the headline gram claim. |

Plant protein is not "bad protein." Many plant proteins are limited by one amino acid: pea is often lower in sulfur amino acids, while rice is often lower in lysine. A blend can make those weak spots less dominant, which is why the source list matters.

India is a useful stress test for this label problem. Protein has moved from gym shelves into daily packaged food: powders, bars, high-protein atta, shakes, yogurts, and dairy upgrades. Grand View Research estimated India's protein supplements market at USD 1.03 billion in 2025 and projected USD 2.71 billion by 2033. Mint reported in February 2026 that whey-cost pressure and import dependence were pushing some Indian companies toward plant-based alternatives.

That boom brings a label problem. A 2024 *Medicine* study by Philips and colleagues tested 36 protein supplements sold in India. The team reported gaps between measured protein and label or ad claims, plus concerns about amino spiking, contaminants, and complex herbal blends. A 2025 *Medicine* follow-up tested 18 medical whey powders and 16 consumer nutrition whey powders sold in India. The consumer nutrition powders averaged 75.6g protein per 100g, while the medical powders averaged 29.1g per 100g; taurine-based nitrogen spiking was found in 89% of medical products versus 12.5% of consumer nutrition products.

Protein spiking is possible because common protein tests measure nitrogen. US labelling law allows protein grams to be calculated as 6.25 times nitrogen content, and FDA protein % Daily Value still uses a corrected protein amount rather than DIAAS. Kjeldahl and Dumas methods are useful nitrogen tests. On their own, they cannot tell intact whey or pea protein from added nitrogen-rich compounds such as taurine, glycine, creatine, urea, or other non-protein nitrogen sources. That is how a label can look strong while the amino acid profile stays thin.

## The Catch

DIAAS is not perfect. Many values come from pig or rat models because human ileal testing is hard to do. Scores change with heat, extrusion, isolation, fermentation, and the whole food matrix. A lentil dal, a lentil flour snack, and a lentil protein isolate are not the same biological product. Current reviews treat DIAAS as a better tool than PDCAAS, not a flawless consumer label.

Lower DIAAS does not mean "bad food." Lentils bring fibre, minerals, polyphenols, and slow-digesting carbohydrate. The nutrition value of a mixed meal is not erased by a lower protein-quality score. The problem starts when a packaged product sells 25g protein as if every source, blend, and nitrogen count were equivalent.

## Practical Monday Action

A useful label routine has three steps. Start with the protein source: whey isolate, whey concentrate, soy isolate, pea, rice, lentil, or blend. Then look for a full amino acid profile, especially leucine, lysine, methionine plus cysteine, threonine, tryptophan, and valine. Finally, read the ingredient list for taurine, glycine, creatine, glutamine, or a "proprietary amino blend" sitting beside the protein source.

The front says grams. The back shows whether the grams have evidence behind them.

## Label Signals

| Signal | What it usually means |
|---|---|
| Named protein source plus full amino acid profile | Clearer label signal |
| Soy isolate or deliberately blended plant profile | Stronger plant-protein signal |
| Pea, rice, lentil, wheat, or single-source plant powder | Needs context from the amino acid profile |
| Headline protein plus proprietary amino blend | Worth a closer read |
| Batch testing and amino acid profile published by product | Better transparency signal |

## The 30-Second Protein Label Read

| Label area | Better signal | Watch signal | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Protein source | Whey isolate, milk protein, soy isolate, or named blend | "Protein blend" without clear proportions | Source drives amino acid quality. |
| Amino profile | Full aminogram with leucine, lysine, methionine plus cysteine, threonine, tryptophan, and valine | Only total protein grams | Total grams do not show quality. |
| Added amino acids | Clearly explained functional reason | Taurine, glycine, creatine, glutamine, or proprietary amino blend beside the protein source | Nitrogen-rich add-ons can inflate protein math. |
| Testing | Batch test, third-party test, or amino profile by product | Big front claim with no supporting data | Transparency is the signal. |
| Plant formula | Soy isolate or deliberate pea-rice style blend | Single-source plant powder with no amino data | Blending can cover weak amino acid spots. |
