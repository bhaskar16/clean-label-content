# Read the ingredient list like a ranked formula

The back of a cereal box, biscuit pack, or flavoured milk bottle can look honest and still make you work. In Codex, US, EU, India, and Australia/New Zealand rules, ingredients are generally listed by weight when they go into the food. First means most. Last means least. The catch is simple: sweetness can show up as sugar, syrup, honey, concentrate, or another name in the same formula.

**CleanLabel verdict:** The first five ingredients tell you what the product mostly is. The full list tells you whether one ingredient family, especially sweeteners, has been split into several smaller-looking names.

## The science

Ingredient lists are not ranked by health value. They are ranked by weight. Codex CXS 1-1985 says ingredients should be listed from most to least by ingoing weight. US rule 21 CFR 101.4 uses the same basic idea. EU Regulation 1169/2011, India's FSSAI Labelling and Display Regulations, and Australia/New Zealand Standard 1.2.4 use similar weight-order rules, with their own details for small amounts and mixed ingredients.

That makes the top of the list useful, but not complete. In the US, ingredients at 2% or less can be grouped after a phrase such as "contains 2% or less of." Once that line appears, the tail is not a precise rank. Codex and FSSAI both give lighter rules for some mixed ingredients under 5% of the finished food, unless additives or other required items need to be named.

Sugar is where this gets easy to misread. The FDA's added-sugars definition includes sugars added during processing. It also includes sugars from syrups and honey, plus some sugars from fruit or vegetable juice concentrates. The Institute of Medicine's 1990 report on nutrition labels flagged the same problem from another angle. When simple sugars are split across names, the full sugar picture can be harder to see.

Consumer label tests point in the same direction, but they are tests of understanding, not health outcomes. Varela et al. published a 2014 eye-tracking study in *Food Research International* with 50 people looking at cereal packs. Few read the nutrition label and ingredient list in that task. Khandpur et al. published a 2017 randomized online test in *Appetite* with 2,509 US adults. Added-sugar meaning was clearer when labels used easier formats, such as grams plus high/medium/low text. In a 2020 randomized online test in the *Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics*, Khandpur et al. studied 1,156 US adults. Readers understood added sugars better on the updated US Nutrition Facts label than on the old one.

## The rule, plainly

Codex sets the global baseline: ingredients are generally listed from most to least by ingoing weight. The US, EU, India, and Australia/New Zealand all use the same broad idea, but each system has its own details.

| Place | Plain-English rule | What to notice |
|---|---|---|
| Codex | Ingredients are generally listed from most to least by ingoing weight. | It is the global baseline, not a full national law. |
| United States | Ingredients are listed by weight; the 2% line can group small amounts. | After the 2% line, the tail is small print, not exact rank. |
| European Union | Ingredients are listed by weight at time of use, with Annex VII exceptions. | Items under 2% may be treated differently after the rest. |
| India | FSSAI requires ingredients to be listed by descending composition by weight or volume at manufacture. | Read ingredient order with the nutrition panel; added sugars must appear there too. |
| Australia/New Zealand | Standard 1.2.4 uses weight-order logic and mixed-ingredient rules. | Same broad idea, different details. |

For Indian labels, the best read is two-part: check the ingredient order, then check the nutrition panel.

## The catch

The ranked list is still one of the best tools on the package. It is not decoration. If sugar, refined flour, or oil appears first, the formula has already said something useful.

But a long list is not automatically a health warning. Some additives work at tiny levels. Some products need stabilizers for texture or shelf life. Maltodextrin needs care too. Under US rules, it is a nonsweet starch-based saccharide polymer, not table sugar. In a label-reading exercise, maltodextrin matters most when it sits near glucose syrup, dextrose, fructose, fruit juice concentrate, or other sweetener-family names. The pattern is the point, not one isolated word.

These are not product verdicts. They are selected label snapshots from official product pages, checked as examples. Formulas can change by country and reformulation, so the package in hand is always the controlling source.

| Product | Selected label signal | What it teaches |
|---|---|---|
| Nutella Classic | Sugar appears first, followed by palm oil, hazelnuts, skim milk, cocoa, lecithin, and vanillin. | "Hazelnut spread" is front-pack identity; the ranked list shows sugar as the largest ingredient by weight. |

## Practical Monday Action

A useful shelf check has three passes. Start with the first five ingredients and name what the food mostly is. Then scan the full list for ingredient families: sugar, sucrose, glucose syrup, corn syrup, dextrose, fructose, maltose, honey, fruit juice concentrate, rice syrup, invert sugar, and syrup names. Last, notice whether the list shifts into a "contains 2% or less" section or a similar small-quantity tail.

The quietest part of the label often explains the loudest claim on the front.
