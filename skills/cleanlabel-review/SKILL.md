# CleanLabel° Pre-Publication Review Strategy

## How this skill relates to the writing skill

This skill is downstream of `cleanlabel-newsletter` (the writing and research skill). The writing skill governs how content is created. This skill governs whether content is allowed to publish. They are used in sequence:

1. Pre-Write Brief (this skill, Section 1) → completed before writing begins
2. Article drafted (writing skill)
3. Gates 1–6 (this skill, Section 2) → completed before publishing

Do not run the gates on a piece that has not had a Pre-Write Brief approved. If a Pre-Write Brief is missing, request one before reviewing.

---

## The CleanLabel° Publication Standard — 15 Tenets

These are the non-negotiable standards every piece of content must meet. They underpin every gate in this skill.

**1. Source verifiability.** Every factual claim traces to a named primary source: a journal paper (with DOI or URL), a regulatory database entry, a named researcher's documented public statement, or a nationally published dataset. If the receipt does not exist, the claim does not appear.

**2. Scientific accuracy.** The science is described as the science actually is — not as it would be convenient to describe it. Animal studies are not presented as human findings. Correlations are not presented as causal relationships. Small trials are not presented as consensus evidence.

**3. Evidence grade transparency.** Every study cited is accompanied by language that matches its actual evidence strength. The grade (RCT, observational, animal model, review) is either stated explicitly or embedded in the phrasing. Readers must be able to calibrate how confident to be in every finding.

**4. Relatability over authority.** The article opens with something the reader has experienced, felt, or wondered — not with a study or a statistic. The science arrives as the explanation for something already felt, not as the premise the reader is expected to accept.

**5. No raw data in prose.** Any numerical finding that requires context to be understood must be visualised — as a table, chart, traffic-light graphic, or comparison infographic. Raw statistics in paragraph form without visual support fail this standard.

**6. Human voice throughout.** The article sounds like it was written by a specific, thoughtful person sitting at a desk. It does not sound like it was generated. Specific AI-pattern signals — symmetrical structures, "Furthermore/Moreover/In conclusion" transitions, restatement summaries, lists of exactly three parallel items at every turn — are treated as failures requiring revision.

**7. Non-clinical tone.** The article is journalism and education, not medicine. It never instructs a specific person to make a specific health decision. It reports what researchers found. It does not advise what readers should do as a result of those findings.

**8. No false urgency or alarm.** Language implying immediate danger ("stop eating this now," "this is harming you") is not permitted unless a regulatory body has issued a recall or ban. The register is "worth knowing" and "the evidence suggests," not "dangerous" and "you must."

**9. Balance of evidence.** Where studies conflict, both sides are represented. Where industry funding introduces a conflict of interest, it is noted. The article cannot cherry-pick evidence that supports a conclusion and ignore evidence that complicates it.

**10. Temporal accuracy.** Claims are anchored to when the evidence was produced. Research from the 1990s that has been substantially revised is either updated or noted as historical context. The publication date of every cited study is included.

**11. Readability at 8th grade level.** Target Flesch-Kincaid score: 60–70. No more than three consecutive sentences of dense technical content without a plain-language break, a concrete example, or a human moment.

**12. Voice consistency.** The CleanLabel° voice has three registers — The Informed Friend, The Investigative Cook, The Calm Alarm — and every paragraph must fit one of them. No paragraph slips into academic, promotional, fear-mongering, or brand-apologetic registers.

**13. No diagnosis, no prescription.** The article cannot recommend a specific dose, a specific product for a specific health condition, or a specific dietary change tailored to a specific person. It reports findings at the population level and attributes them to researchers, not to CleanLabel°.

**14. Brand name discipline.** Named brands mentioned in a negative or cautionary context must have a documented, citable evidential basis — a regulatory action, a published lab test, or a peer-reviewed study that specifically names the product. If the receipt does not exist, the brand is not named. Generic category descriptions are used instead.

**15. The five-years-from-now test.** Claims that sit in grey areas of evolving science are hedged with temporal language: "current evidence suggests," "as of 2026, researchers believe," "this is an emerging area." Content must age gracefully even if the science shifts.

## Section 1 — Pre-Write Brief

**This section must be completed and approved before any article is drafted.**

The Pre-Write Brief is a structured document the writer fills out before a single sentence of the article is written. Its purpose is to confirm that the article has a defensible scientific foundation, a clear audience, and no structural problems before time is invested in the draft. A brief that cannot be completed usually means the article idea is not ready — and that is better discovered before writing than after.

### How to use the Pre-Write Brief

The writer completes all fields. The editor reviews the brief. If the brief is approved, writing begins. If any field is incomplete or weak, the brief is returned with specific notes. Writing does not start until the brief is approved.

When running this review as an agent: if asked to review an article without a Pre-Write Brief attached, generate the brief fields from the article content and note which fields are missing or weak. Flag these as risks before proceeding to the gates.

### Brief approval criteria

Approve the brief if:
- All fields are complete
- The core claim is specific, falsifiable, and supported by at least two Tier 1 sources
- The hook moment is specific and reader-centred, not a statistic
- All brand names in cautionary context have documented evidential bases
- No legal flags are unanswered or unresolved
- At least one data visual is planned for any article with numerical findings

Return the brief with notes if:
- The core claim is vague ("this ingredient is bad for you") or unsourced
- Any primary source is missing the study type, sample size, or DOI
- A brand is named in a cautionary context without a documented evidential basis
- A legal flag is answered "yes" without a resolution stated
- The hook moment is a statistic rather than a human experience
- No visual is planned despite the article containing numerical findings

## Section 2 — The Six Review Gates

Run these gates in sequence after the article is drafted. An article that fails any gate is returned to the writer with specific notes. It does not advance to the next gate until the failing gate is passed.

---

### Gate 1 — Source Verification Pass

**Purpose:** Confirm every factual claim in the article has a documented source.
**Time budget:** 30–45 minutes per article.
**Who runs it:** Researcher or editor.

#### Process

Go through the article sentence by sentence. Every sentence that makes a factual claim gets flagged for verification. A claim is any assertion about:
- A study finding (what it found, who conducted it, what the sample was)
- A regulatory body's position, rule, or permitted level
- A researcher's view or public statement
- A statistic, percentage, or numerical assertion
- A historical fact presented as relevant to the article's argument

Each flagged claim must be matched to one of three valid backing types:

**Type A — Named primary source.** Author, year, journal, sample size, DOI or URL. Verified by checking the actual source, not relying on the draft's description.

**Type B — Direct quote with attribution.** The researcher's exact words, attributed to a specific documented context (paper, interview, public talk, published book).

**Type C — Explicit uncertainty statement.** Where the evidence does not exist or is genuinely contested, the article explicitly says so: "researchers are investigating," "evidence is mixed," "no consensus has been established."

#### Automatic Gate 1 failures

Flag any of the following and return to writer before proceeding:

- "Studies show" — without naming which study
- A percentage or statistic with no source
- A regulatory claim ("FSSAI allows X," "FDA bans Y") without the specific regulation cited
- A named researcher's view without a specific paper, interview, or public statement sourced
- A claim framed as consensus without a systematic review or meta-analysis supporting it
- A study described as finding X when the actual finding was an association with X (causation vs correlation)
- A study's finding applied to a population different from the one studied (e.g. mouse findings applied to humans, or a US population finding applied to India, without acknowledgement)

#### Gate 1 output

Produce a verified claim map: a list of every flagged claim with its verification status (Verified / Unverified / Requires hedging). Submit alongside the marked-up draft. Gate 1 passes when zero Unverified claims remain.

---

### Gate 2 — Scientific Accuracy and Evidence Grade Review

**Purpose:** Confirm the science is correctly described and that the language used matches the actual strength of the evidence.
**Time budget:** 45–60 minutes per article.
**Who runs it:** Editor with science background, or external reviewer.

#### Five accuracy questions for every major claim

**Q1 — Is the study described accurately?**
Check the original abstract. Confirm the finding described in the article matches what the study actually reported. The most common error: describing an association as a proven effect, or a secondary outcome as a primary finding.

**Q2 — Is the study design correctly characterised?**
An RCT is not an observational study. A cohort study is not a randomised trial. A meta-analysis is not a single paper. Every cited study must be correctly typed in the article, either explicitly or through the language used to describe it.

**Q3 — Is the sample size present?**
A finding from n=16 and a finding from n=11,000 both have a place in journalism. They do not have equal weight. The sample size must be stated or the confidence of the surrounding language must be adjusted to match.

**Q4 — Is the finding current?**
Check the publication year. For any study older than 10 years, search for significant replications, contradictions, or meta-analyses that have since updated the finding. If the original finding has been substantially revised, the article must reflect the current state of the evidence.

**Q5 — Are limitations acknowledged?**
Every study has limitations. The article need not list all of them, but the most significant one — the one most relevant to how confidently the reader should take the finding — must appear.

#### Evidence grade matrix

Apply this matrix to every study cited. The language in the article must match the permitted column for the study type. If it uses language from the not-permitted column, revise before passing Gate 2.

| Study type | Language permitted | Language NOT permitted |
|---|---|---|
| Single animal / in vitro study | "In animal models..." / "Cell studies suggest..." / "Preliminary research indicates..." | "Research shows..." / "Scientists found..." / "Evidence confirms..." |
| Single observational study | "A study of [n] people found an association between..." / "Researchers observed a correlation..." | "Eating X causes Y" / "X is linked to Y" (without qualifying "in this study") |
| Small RCT (n < 50) | "A preliminary trial found..." / "One small randomised study reported..." / "Early evidence from a trial suggests..." | "Evidence shows..." / "Research confirms..." / "Studies prove..." |
| Large RCT (n ≥ 200) | "A randomised trial found..." / "The evidence suggests..." / "Controlled research found..." | "Eating X causes Y" (causation still needs mechanistic evidence, not just RCT correlation) |
| Systematic review / meta-analysis | "A review of [n] studies found..." / "The research consistently shows..." / "Across multiple studies..." | "Proven" / "Definitive" / "Certain" |
| Established scientific consensus | State with confidence. | No restrictions beyond accuracy. |
| Industry-funded study | Must note funding source. Use language from the appropriate study type above, plus "though the study was funded by [entity]" | Presenting as independent evidence without disclosing funding |

#### Gate 2 output

Comment on every major claim: evidence grade, whether the language matches, and any revisions needed. Gate 2 passes when all claims use language matching their evidence grade.

### Gate 3 — Relatability and Voice Review

**Purpose:** Confirm the article reads like a human wrote it, sounds like CleanLabel°, and will be read to the end by the target reader.
**Time budget:** 30 minutes per article.
**Who runs it:** Editor with audience knowledge.

#### The three-paragraph test

Read the first three paragraphs aloud. By the end of paragraph three, the reader should have felt recognised — a moment of "I know this feeling" or "this is about me." If the article opens with a study, a statistic, or a claim rather than a human moment, it has failed this test. Return to writer with the note: the hook is not landing.

#### The real person check

Read the article as the person described in the opening hook. Answer these four questions:

1. Does the science explain something this person was confused about?
2. Does the article end with something this person can actually do or think about differently?
3. Does the tone feel like a knowledgeable friend rather than a textbook?
4. Does the article treat this person as intelligent rather than in need of instruction?

If any answer is no, identify the specific paragraphs that fail and return with revision notes.

#### The density test

Count consecutive sentences containing technical terminology, study names, or numerical data. If five or more appear in a row without a plain-language break, a concrete example, or a human moment, flag for revision. The rule: no reader should have to work hard for more than three consecutive sentences.

#### Voice register check

Every paragraph must fit one of the three CleanLabel° voice registers:

**The Informed Friend** — warm, direct, never condescending. Treats the reader as intelligent and time-poor. Never uses jargon without immediately explaining it in the same sentence or the next. Never makes the reader feel judged for not already knowing something.

**The Investigative Cook** — shows its work. Names sources. Doesn't assert without evidence. The reader can always see the receipt. This register sounds confident because it is backed, not because it is assertive.

**The Calm Alarm** — when something concerning is reported, the language is precise and measured. "This is worth knowing" not "this is dangerous." "Researchers have flagged concerns" not "this is harming you." Never catastrophises. Never minimises.

Flag any paragraph that slips into:
- Academic register ("it has been demonstrated that," "the evidence base suggests")
- Promotional register ("incredible," "game-changing," "you need this")
- Fear-mongering register ("this is destroying," "you are being poisoned," "stop immediately")
- Brand-apologetic register ("while it may seem concerning, the overall evidence is reassuring")

#### AI pattern detection

Read every paragraph looking specifically for these signals. Any match is a revision flag:

| Pattern | Example | Why it flags |
|---|---|---|
| Symmetrical three-part lists everywhere | "First... Second... Third..." in every section | Structural marker of generated text |
| Connective transitions | "Furthermore," "Moreover," "In addition," "It is worth noting that," "It is important to understand" | No human writer of this voice uses these |
| Summary restatement | A paragraph that restates what the previous paragraph just said | Padding pattern of generated text |
| False balance framing | "On one hand... On the other hand..." used repeatedly | Mechanical structure, not human reasoning |
| Hedge stacking | "It may be possible that some evidence suggests..." | Over-hedging to avoid commitment — not the CleanLabel° voice |
| Uniform sentence length | Every sentence 18–22 words | Rhythm is a giveaway; humans vary more |
| Generic opener | "In today's world..." / "Many people wonder..." / "When it comes to..." | Classic generated filler openers |

The test for any flagged paragraph: could this paragraph have been written by a specific person, sitting at a desk, thinking this through for the first time? If yes, it passes. If it could plausibly have been generated, it fails.

#### Gate 3 output

A paragraph-level annotation: each paragraph marked Pass, Revise (with reason), or Rewrite (with direction). Gate 3 passes when no paragraph is marked Rewrite and fewer than 20% are marked Revise.

### Gate 4 — Data Visualisation and Infographic Review

**Purpose:** Confirm that no raw data appears unsupported in prose, and that all numerical findings are accompanied by a visual.
**Time budget:** 20–30 minutes per article.
**Who runs it:** Editor and designer.

#### The raw data rule

No number in the article should require the reader to do mental work to understand its significance. Every number must do one of two things:

**Option A — Anchor comparison.** The number is immediately followed by a reference the reader already knows. Example: "26g of sugar — the same as 6.5 teaspoons, and above the WHO's 25g daily free-sugar limit."

**Option B — Visual support.** The number is part of a finding that is displayed in a table, chart, or infographic. The prose points to the visual; the visual provides the full context.

Numbers that do neither fail Gate 4.

#### Mandatory visual checklist

Every article that contains any of the following must have the corresponding visual:

| Content trigger | Required visual |
|---|---|
| Named ingredient reviewed for safety | Traffic-light status card (Clean / Watch / Avoid with one-line reason) |
| Two or more things compared (e.g. DIAAS vs PDCAAS, FDA vs FSSAI) | Comparison table |
| A percentage, before/after measurement, or ranked finding | Chart, infographic, or illustrated data point |
| A product brand audit with scores | Scoring rubric table with product results |
| A timeline of events or regulatory history | Timeline graphic |
| A mechanism (how something works in the body) | Illustrated process diagram |

#### Visual accuracy checklist

For every visual in the article:

- Every number in the visual matches the number in the cited source — check against the original
- Every label uses terminology consistent with the source study — not paraphrased in a way that changes meaning
- Every colour assignment is consistent with the CleanLabel° traffic-light system: sage green for clean/established/safe, amber for watch/uncertain/mixed evidence, red for avoid/concerning
- The data source is credited on or beneath the visual
- The visual does not make the finding appear more alarming or more certain than the article's language does

#### Visual tone check

A visual that uses red prominently for a finding the article describes as "preliminary" or "uncertain" has failed this gate. The visual must not contradict the evidence grade communicated in the text. If the article says "one small study suggested," the visual cannot use AVOID-level red styling.

#### Gate 4 output

A visual review log: each visual listed with pass/fail status and any corrections required. Gate 4 passes when all mandatory visuals are present and all visual accuracy checks pass.

---

### Gate 5 — Legal and Liability Review

**Purpose:** Confirm the article contains no content that creates realistic legal exposure for CleanLabel°.
**Time budget:** 20 minutes per article.
**Who runs it:** Editor-in-chief or designated legal reviewer.

#### The three primary legal risk questions

**Question 1 — Does the article make a claim about a specific named product or brand that is not supported by a documented, citable evidential basis?**

Permissible: reporting on a named product's ingredient list, regulatory status, or results of a published third-party lab test.

Not permissible: asserting that a named product is unsafe, harmful, fraudulent, or inferior to competitors based on the article's own editorial opinion rather than documented evidence.

If yes: either supply the documented evidential basis or replace the brand name with a generic category description.

**Question 2 — Does the article, at any point, give specific dietary advice?**

Dietary advice means: a second-person instruction ("you should eat X," "avoid Y if you have Z," "take X mg of Y daily") framed as a recommendation rather than a report of what researchers found.

Permissible: "Researchers in the Wastyk et al. study found that participants who consumed fermented foods daily showed increased microbiome diversity."

Not permissible: "You should eat fermented foods daily to improve your microbiome."

If yes: reframe every instance as a report of what researchers found, not an instruction to the reader.

**Question 3 — Does the article reproduce any third-party intellectual property without clear permission or fair use basis?**

This includes: journal paper figures or charts reproduced directly, brand logos, product photographs taken by others, text excerpts exceeding fair use limits.

Fair use in editorial journalism generally covers: quoting briefly from a public statement, showing a product label photograph taken by the publication itself, referencing a study's published findings in your own words.

If yes: obtain permission or replace with original content.

#### The brand name audit

For every brand name mentioned in a negative, cautionary, or comparative context, confirm:

1. The specific evidential basis (regulatory action number, published study DOI, lab test source)
2. The evidential basis is cited in the article
3. The claim made about the brand is an accurate description of the evidence, not an extension of it

If any brand fails this audit, remove the brand name and use a category description instead. Do not publish a named brand claim without a documented receipt.

#### The disclaimer audit

Confirm the standard CleanLabel° disclaimer is present in the correct form:

**Standard disclaimer text:**
*"This article reports on peer-reviewed scientific research published in named journals. All findings are attributed to their source studies and researchers. CleanLabel° is not a medical publication and does not provide health advice. Nothing in this article constitutes a diagnosis, treatment recommendation, or substitute for consultation with a qualified healthcare professional."*

Confirm:
- Disclaimer is present in the article
- Disclaimer text has not been shortened or altered
- Font size is no smaller than 8pt (in PDF / print formats)
- Colour is not so light as to be unreadable
- In multi-page formats, the disclaimer appears on at least the first and last page

#### The final legal scan

Read the article one complete time looking only for sentences that could be read as:
- An assertion about a brand or person that the article's evidential basis does not fully support
- A claim about health outcomes that implies certainty the evidence does not support
- A recommendation to a specific type of person about a specific health action
- Reproduction of content that requires permission

Any sentence matching any of these is flagged for revision or removal before Gate 5 passes.

#### Gate 5 output

A legal review log: each of the three primary questions answered, the brand name audit results, the disclaimer audit results, and a list of any flagged sentences with revision notes. Gate 5 passes only when all three questions are answered cleanly, all brand names have documented evidential bases, and the disclaimer is present and correct.

### Gate 6 — Final Publication Check

**Purpose:** Confirm the article is publication-ready across all dimensions before it goes live.
**Time budget:** 15 minutes per article.
**Who runs it:** Editor-in-chief or final approver.

This gate is not a content review. It is a final readiness confirmation. All Gate 1–5 issues must be resolved and documented before Gate 6 begins.

#### The 12-point final checklist

All 12 must be confirmed. Any unconfirmed item sends the article back to the relevant gate.

**Content accuracy**
- [ ] All Gate 1–5 comments are resolved and signed off by the editor who raised them
- [ ] The article headline accurately represents the content — it is not more alarming, more certain, or more definitive than the article supports
- [ ] All study citations include: author surname, year, journal name, and DOI or URL
- [ ] The evidence grade of the article's central claim is correctly represented in the language throughout

**Science and voice**
- [ ] No paragraph uses language from the "not permitted" column of the evidence grade matrix (Gate 2)
- [ ] The article has been checked for AI-pattern language and all flagged paragraphs revised (Gate 3)
- [ ] The Flesch-Kincaid readability score is between 55–70 (run through a readability tool)

**Visuals and data**
- [ ] All mandatory visuals are present and sourced (Gate 4)
- [ ] No number in the prose appears without an anchor comparison or visual support (Gate 4)

**Legal**
- [ ] The standard disclaimer is present in the correct form and position (Gate 5)
- [ ] All brand names mentioned in cautionary context have documented evidential bases cited in the article (Gate 5)
- [ ] The article ends with a finding, an observation, or a question — not a recommendation

#### Gate 6 output

A signed-off checklist with the name of the final approver and the date. This document is retained as the publication record for the article. If the article is ever challenged, the Gate 6 checklist is the evidence that the review process was completed.

**Article status after Gate 6:** APPROVED TO PUBLISH.

| Gate | Owner | Time | Trigger |

| Gate 2 — Scientific Accuracy | Editor (science background) | 45–60 min | Gate 1 passed |

For a solo operation (one person playing all roles), run each gate on a separate day with a full break between them. Reviewing your own work in the same session you wrote it almost always fails Gate 3.

## Section 4 — Failure Reference Card

Quick-reference for the most common failure modes at each gate, and what to do when they occur.

| Gate | Most common failure | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-Write Brief | Core claim is vague or unsourced | Return to writer: "Restate the claim as a specific, falsifiable assertion and identify the primary source that supports it." |
| Gate 1 | "Studies show" without citation | Return with: "Name the study. Author, year, journal, sample size." |
| Gate 1 | Regulatory claim without regulation cited | Return with: "Cite the specific FSSAI / FDA / EFSA database entry or document." |
| Gate 2 | Animal study presented as human evidence | Revise to: "In animal models, researchers found..." |
| Gate 2 | Correlation described as causation | Revise to: "An association was found between X and Y in [study]." |
| Gate 2 | Industry-funded study not disclosed | Add: "though the study was funded by [entity]" |
| Gate 3 | Opens with a statistic, not a human moment | Return with: "Rewrite the opening. Start with a scene or feeling the reader has experienced." |
| Gate 3 | "Furthermore / Moreover / In conclusion" | Rewrite the transition. Find the natural human thought that connects the paragraphs. |
| Gate 3 | Five or more consecutive technical sentences | Add a plain-language break: a concrete example, a relatable comparison, or a one-sentence human observation. |
| Gate 4 | Raw percentage in prose without visual | Create the visual or add an anchor comparison immediately after the number. |
| Gate 4 | Visual uses red styling for preliminary finding | Revise visual to amber or neutral styling. |
| Gate 5 | Named brand in cautionary context without evidential basis | Remove brand name. Use category description: "at least one major Indian protein brand," "a leading US breakfast cereal." |
| Gate 5 | "You should eat X" or "avoid Y if you have Z" | Reframe: "The researchers found that participants who ate X showed Y outcome." |
| Gate 5 | Disclaimer missing | Add the standard disclaimer text before Gate 6 review. |
| Gate 6 | Headline is more alarming than the article supports | Revise headline to match the article's actual evidence grade. |
| Gate 6 | Article ends with a recommendation | Revise the ending to a finding, observation, or open question. |

## Section 5 — Standard Disclaimer Text

This is the approved text. It must not be shortened, paraphrased, or altered. It appears at the bottom of every article and in the top callout box of any article that discusses health outcomes, ingredients, or dietary patterns.

**Full version (for callout boxes and article footers):**

> *This article reports on peer-reviewed scientific research published in named journals. All findings are attributed to their source studies and researchers. CleanLabel° is not a medical publication and does not provide health advice. Nothing in this article constitutes a diagnosis, treatment recommendation, or substitute for consultation with a qualified healthcare professional. If you are experiencing symptoms or have a health concern, speak to a registered doctor or dietitian.*

**Short version (for email footers and PDF footers only):**

> *For informational purposes only. Not medical advice. CleanLabel° reports on published research — it does not provide health recommendations. Consult a qualified healthcare professional before making dietary changes.*

The short version may only be used in formats where the full version has already appeared (e.g. the full version appears in the article body; the short version appears in the email footer of the same issue).
