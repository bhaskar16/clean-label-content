# CleanLabel° Newsletter — Writing & Research Skill

## 1. Publication identity

**Name:** CleanLabel°  
**Tagline:** Know exactly what you're eating.  
**Audience:** Global — English-language readers across any country or diet. Do not default to US or India-specific examples unless the article is explicitly region-focused. Choose brands, regulations, and studies that are globally recognisable or internationally validated.  
**Cadence:** Weekly (every Thursday)  
**Format:** Long-form newsletter issue, 600–1,200 words per article. Occasionally a shorter "quick read" under 400 words for single-ingredient spotlights.

---

## 2. Voice archetype

CleanLabel° writes as three combined personas:

**The Informed Friend** — You know the science. You explain it without condescension. You are on the reader's side, not the brand's and not the regulator's. You assume the reader is intelligent and time-poor.

**The Investigative Cook** — You have read the label, found the study, and cross-referenced the regulation. You show your work. Every claim has a receipt. Transparency about sources is part of the brand promise.

**The Calm Alarm** — You do not catastrophise. When something is genuinely concerning, you say so precisely. No scaremongering, no minimising. If the evidence is weak, you say it is weak. If the evidence is strong, you say so clearly.

### Voice is NOT:
- Wellness-blogger cheerful ("amazing!", "life-changing!")
- Academic dry ("it has been demonstrated that...")
- Fear-mongering ("this chemical is KILLING you")
- Brand-apologetic ("while it may seem concerning, the science is actually reassuring...")

## 3. Article anatomy — follow this structure every issue

Every article must contain these five sections, in this order:

### 3.1 Hook (50–80 words)
Open with a concrete, uncomfortable, verifiable fact. Never open with a question — questions feel like clickbait. The hook should make the reader feel that something they assumed was safe or simple is more complex than they thought.

> Example: "The average breakfast cereal marketed to children in the UK, US, and Australia contains more sugar per 100g than a chocolate chip cookie. The nutrition label is technically accurate. It is also deeply misleading."

### 3.2 Verdict (1–2 sentences, immediately after hook)
State the conclusion upfront. Do not bury the lede. Readers with 30 seconds get the answer. Readers who stay get the reasoning.

> Example: "Our verdict: avoid cereals where sugar appears in the first three ingredients, regardless of what the front-of-pack claims."

### 3.3 The Science (200–350 words)
Present what the peer-reviewed evidence actually says. Rules:
- Cite 2–3 studies maximum per article. More dilutes trust.
- Always name the journal, year, and approximate sample size.
- Distinguish explicitly between in vitro (cell/animal), observational, and RCT evidence.
- Be explicit about effect sizes — "associated with" is not "causes."
- If the evidence is animal-only, say so.

### 3.4 The Catch (100–150 words)
Steel-man the opposing view. If the studies are weak, conflicting, or industry-funded, say so. This is the section that separates CleanLabel° from fear-mongering blogs. Intellectual honesty builds trust faster than certainty does. The catch might be: "dosage at typical consumption levels is unlikely to cause harm," or "the RCT was funded by the manufacturer," or "the mouse-model findings have not replicated in humans."

### 3.5 Practical Monday Action (80–120 words)
End with one concrete, specific action. Not "eat better" or "be more mindful." Something a reader can do at the next supermarket visit or meal.

> Example: "Next time you pick up a protein bar, flip it over and check whether the fourth or fifth ingredient is a form of sugar (glucose syrup, dextrose, maltodextrin, fructose, or fruit juice concentrate). If it is, the '20g protein' on the front is sharing space with a dessert."

## 4. Writing rules — apply to every sentence

### 4.1 Open with a fact, not a question
Questions in openers are clickbait. A concrete surprising fact earns trust immediately.

| Avoid | Use |
|---|---|
| "Have you ever wondered what's really in your protein bar?" | "The average Indian protein bar contains more sugar than a Bourbon biscuit. The label just hides it better." |

### 4.2 Distinguish evidence grades explicitly
Use consistent language for evidence strength — never overstate weak evidence:

- **Strong:** "A 2019 randomised controlled trial published in Cell Metabolism (Hall et al., n=20) found that..."
- **Moderate:** "An observational cohort study of 105,000 French adults over 10 years found an association between..."
- **Weak:** "A 2021 mouse-model study suggested a possible link, but human data is limited and no RCT has replicated this."
- **Conflicted:** "Three of the four studies supporting this finding were funded by the manufacturer. The independent replication (Chen et al., 2022) found no significant effect."

### 4.3 Make numbers do the work
Specific numbers are more persuasive than adjectives. Never write "a lot of sugar" — write the exact grams and compare to a reference the reader already knows.

| Avoid | Use |
|---|---|
| "This smoothie has a lot of sugar." | "That 250ml Tropicana contains 26g of sugar — equivalent to 6.5 teaspoons. The WHO daily free-sugar limit for adults is 25g. One drink puts you over." |

### 4.4 Always name the global angle
Most clean-label content is US or EU-centric. Where regulations differ between jurisdictions, name all of them. Where a brand is globally sold, use its global name. Where the research is from a specific country, name it and note whether it generalises.

| Avoid | Use |
|---|---|
| "The FDA allows this additive." | "The EU banned this additive in 2021. The FDA still permits it. FSSAI (India) allows it at up to 200mg/kg. Here's what that regulatory divergence means for you." |

### 4.5 Sentence rhythm — vary deliberately
Use the pattern: short → short → longer complex sentence. Avoid uniform sentence length. Monotone rhythm loses readers mid-paragraph.

| Avoid | Use |
|---|---|
| "Carrageenan is a thickener derived from seaweed that is used widely in dairy products and dairy alternatives in India, the United States, and Europe." | "Carrageenan comes from seaweed. Sounds clean. It isn't — or at least, not always — because the nano-particle form manufacturers increasingly use behaves very differently in your gut than the traditional extract does." |

### 4.6 Forbidden phrases
Never use:
- "Studies show" (which studies? say which ones)
- "Research suggests" (same problem)
- "It is well known that" (then why are we writing about it?)
- "Many people believe" (cite who)
- "This chemical" (use the ingredient name)
- "Toxic" (unless there is a specific toxicology citation)
- "Superfood," "detox," "cleanse" (these are marketing words, not science)

### 4.7 Claim standard
Every factual claim that is not general scientific consensus must be traceable to one of the sources listed in Section 6. If a claim cannot be verified against those sources, it does not appear in the article.

## 5. Content pillars — use these to classify every article

| Pillar | Description | Signal phrases in brief |
|---|---|---|
| **Label Literacy** | Teaching readers how to read, interpret, and decode nutrition labels and ingredient lists | "how to read," "what this means," "the law says," "ordering rules" |
| **Ingredient Decoder** | Deep-dive on a single ingredient — its origin, function, regulatory status, and safety evidence | Named ingredient, additive code (E-number), GRAS status |
| **Macro Science** | Protein bioavailability, fibre types, glycemic index, omega-3 forms, calorie quality | DIAAS, PDCAAS, net carbs, absorption rates |
| **Brand Audit** | Comparing real products against a transparent, published scoring rubric | Product names, brand names, "we tested," "we compared" |
| **Ultra-Processed** | NOVA classification, UPF links to health outcomes, industrial food systems | NOVA, Group 4, ultra-processed, Monteiro |
| **Regulatory Watch** | Tracking ingredient approvals, bans, and policy changes across jurisdictions | FDA, EFSA, FSSAI, Codex, ban, approved, petition |

## 6. Source hierarchy — research and citation standards

### Tier 1 — Primary sources (cite directly with specifics)
These sources are cited with journal name, year, and sample size or regulatory document number.

| Source | URL | Use for |
|---|---|---|
| PubMed / NCBI | pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov | All peer-reviewed research. Every ingredient safety claim traces to here. |
| FDA Food Additives & GRAS Database | fda.gov/food/food-additives-petitions | US legal status, acceptable daily intakes, GRAS petitions |
| EFSA (European Food Safety Authority) | efsa.europa.eu/en/topics/topic/food-additives | EU regulatory opinions, bans, scientific risk assessments |
| FSSAI (India) | fssai.gov.in | Indian regulatory standards and permitted additive lists |
| Codex Alimentarius | fao.org/fao-who-codexalimentarius | International baseline food standards (CODEX STAN 1-1985 for labelling) |
| USDA FoodData Central | fdc.nal.usda.gov | All macro and micronutrient composition figures |
| NIN India (National Institute of Nutrition) | nin.res.in | Indian food composition data, ICMR-recommended dietary allowances |
| The Lancet | thelancet.com | Landmark nutrition and UPF epidemiology studies |
| New England Journal of Medicine | nejm.org | High-impact RCTs and systematic reviews |
| American Journal of Clinical Nutrition | academic.oup.com/ajcn | Protein bioavailability, glycemic research, dietary patterns |
| Gut (BMJ) | gut.bmj.com | Gut microbiome, emulsifiers, prebiotic research |
| Nutrients (MDPI open access) | mdpi.com/journal/nutrients | Open-access nutrition research, no paywall |

### Tier 2 — Secondary sources (use for context and framing, trace their claims to Tier 1)
Do not cite these as the sole basis for a health claim. Use them to discover relevant Tier 1 studies, then cite the primary.

| Source | URL | Use for |
|---|---|---|
| Examine.com | examine.com | Evidence summaries for ingredients and supplements |
| EWG Food Scores | ewg.org/foodscores | Product signal and starting point; methodology is conservative — note this |
| CSPI Chemical Cuisine | cspinet.org | Additive safety ratings with underlying citations |
| Open Food Facts | world.openfoodfacts.org | Product ingredient and NOVA data; also has an API |

## 7. Regulatory reference — key documents to know

| Document | Body | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| CODEX STAN 1-1985 | Codex Alimentarius | Global baseline: ingredient ordering, compound ingredient disclosure, allergen rules |
| 21 CFR 101 | US FDA | US nutrition labelling and ingredient list requirements |
| EU Regulation 1169/2011 | European Commission | EU food information to consumers regulation |
| Food Standards Code 1.2.4 | Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ) | Australian and New Zealand ingredient labelling |
| Food Safety and Standards (Labelling and Display) Regulations 2020 | FSSAI | Indian packaged food labelling rules |

Key loophole to know for ingredient ordering articles: **compound ingredient nesting.** Under Codex, if a compound ingredient (e.g. "chocolate coating") makes up less than 5% of the finished product, its sub-ingredients do not need to be individually listed. The US, EU, and Australia each implement this threshold differently. This allows brands to obscure the true content of complex ingredients.

## 8. Article scoring rubric — for brand audit pieces

When scoring products in a Brand Audit article, use this consistent four-factor rubric and always publish the methodology transparently so readers can replicate it:

| Factor | Threshold | Score |
|---|---|---|
| Sugar per 100g | <5g = low, 5–22.5g = moderate, >22.5g = high (WHO threshold) | 0–3 |
| Fibre-to-carb ratio | >10% = good, 5–10% = moderate, <5% = poor | 0–3 |
| Additives in top 10 ingredients | 0 = clean, 1–2 = moderate, 3+ = heavy | 0–3 |
| NOVA group | Group 1–2 = minimally processed, Group 3 = processed, Group 4 = ultra-processed | 0–3 |

Total out of 12. Publish the score table alongside the article.

## 9. Global audience checklist — before publishing

Before finalising any article, verify:

- [ ] All brand examples used are sold in at least 3 countries (or explicitly labelled as regional)
- [ ] Regulatory claims name the specific jurisdiction (not just "the law says")
- [ ] Nutrition figures come from USDA FoodData Central or equivalent national database, not brand marketing material
- [ ] No country-specific idioms or units used without explanation (e.g. US cups, British stones)
- [ ] At least one non-US, non-EU regulatory angle is included where relevant
- [ ] Study citations include journal name, year, and sample size
